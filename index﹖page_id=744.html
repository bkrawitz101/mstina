PART 1 - Technologies for Teaching Investigation:


I’m especially grateful for this assignment because I’ve always wanted to have the skills to make websites, ever since website making became DIY.  I have made a website before, and have been a content provider for most of my professional career in marketing communications.  But those were the days when most had to rely on a graphic designer to build a website from scratch, myself included.  So I’ve been thrilled for technology to finally catch up and be automated for this portion of website making, which now permits anyone to create a website.  This is why I chose website building platforms to explore.


A professional website will allow instructors to set a first impression to hiring schools and students, before meeting in person - virtual or actual.  It is first a sales tool to show the services an instructor can provide.  It’s then also a platform where an instructor will be able to fully present themselves: expressing their personality and style; their credentials, resume and accolades; as well as a portfolio to show samples of their teaching methods and materials.  Advanced level websites can also host lessons, like a personal Canvas system used by UCLA Extension, for this course.  They can even handle online monetary transactions, such as the selling of products, say instructional lessons, materials, and tools.


I have always heard of Wix.com, as a popular free website building platform.  From the assignment description, I learned about Weebly for the first time.  A quick web search showed that these are indeed, the two most popular (mainly free) website builders.  Wix is mostly free, compared to Weebly, but costs more on the monthly paid subscription for higher-end functions.  The monthly fees for either is typical of web service fees ranging from $6-$35, pending the number of features included in the package.  Weebly is $5-$10 less than Wix.  Both also have hundreds of apps available to add even more tools and functions to the websites.  They are additional charges with typical app fees.

PART 2 - Week 1 Translation Assignment Reflection:


For the translation app assignment last week, I used Google Translate to translate a paragraph on what my expectations are for this Technologies course, from English to Chinese.  The overall translation of the subject was correct, and the details of the target language (individual words) were correct too.  


However, there were gross mistakes in the translation as well.  A couple of phrases were translated in the exact opposite meaning.  For example, Google translated “speakers of other languages” to “native English speakers”, which are exact opposites.  Another mistake was translating “I would also like to be introduced…” to “I would also like to introduce…”  With just one word missing, the meaning of this part of the sentence changed completely.


I learned from this assignment that online translations are useful as a dictionary to find the meaning of single words or simple and commonly used phrases, especially say for students of younger ages learning languages at a very basic level.  For students who are older and learning a language with more complex subjects to comprehend and communicate, online translation apps might confuse and be unreliable.  


So pending the age group of the students and complexities of the subjects, translation apps can be used to gain a general understanding of the subject matter and a large part of the meaning of the words in the phrases or paragraphs, translated somewhat accurately.  However, translation apps most often, may not be adequate to correctly translate complex subjects in phrases, and particularly paragraphs of writing.  Instructors will need to caution students of the apps’ limitations.  Exercises like last week’s translation assignment that yielded these results, would be a good first-hand demonstration of these limitations to the students.
