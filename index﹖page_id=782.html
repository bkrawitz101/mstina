Discussion Topic

1.Post the link to the Youtube video of yourself teaching a 4 to 10-minute lesson based on your lesson plan. Follow the guidelines and instructions posted after Week 8 Module.

2. View three of your classmates' videos and  comment on their teaching a lesson. In your comments be guided by what you've learned earlier by observing, planning and teaching a lesson. Make sure to start with two "glows" (things that stood out) and one "grow" (one thing that may be developed further). You can also go back to the six TESOL  Principles that you used in lesson observation, but don't forget that what you watched was just  a snippet of a lesson. 

For this assignment you are going to work in groups of  3-4  so that everyone can get feedback about their teaching. Once you've commented on the lessons in your  group, you are welcome to watch and comment on  the lessons outside your group. Make sure to post in a timely manner - your classmates depend on you.

Hi Gabriela,

I deeply appreciate your encouraging compliments, comments, and suggestions, which helped me understand there may be some misunderstanding to these videos that appeared like animated cartoons. I replied to your similar comment on my Methods class video too but maybe you did not see it.


My videos are filmed in live and completely immersive VR environments. Each student participates in a first-person view as though they are in front of an instructor in physical reality. The ‘video’ was shot in this way to demonstrate how an actual class will operate. These videos are not cartoon animations for students to watch, they are 3D graphic representations of classroom environments, teaching materials, and of the people in them, live and in real-time.  So in essence, the students do not watch a recorded video but participate in a live 3D VR environment, which is available on VR goggles, like Oculus Quest as well as in 2D on a standard computer, both Windows and Mac.  

 

My husband is developing applications that will allow the instructor to instantaneously produce ‘in-world any 3D object that is contained in our library of teaching objects. This app will insert words and objects that match them, for example, the word ‘Apple’ right next to a 3D apple, which the teacher and students will be able to interact with, including moving them around as they would in a video game. He is also planning on creating an entire set of Cuisenaire rods, with the words already printed on them. It will also enable the students to have pens to write within the VR classroom. Tilt Brush is an example of this. 


On showing my face, I purposely try to avoid revealing my ethnic identity, addressing preferential hiring practices in Taiwan and Mainland China for non-East Asian teachers. This is why I chose a Caucasian-looking avatar to represent me. And since the students are in the VR environments with me, and not watching a Zoom chat window or video, unless if I enable a video screen inside of my VR classroom for students to see my real face, they would anyway only be able to see the lower part of my face since my eyes would be covered by the VR goggles headset.


VR has given us a whole new level of possibilities for remote teaching, including being able to teach students globally in fantastical worlds of imagination, where anything could be made possible, fully immersive and interactive without losing social contact, rather than just the one-way video conference type of remote learning. Any teacher will be able to come into our classrooms with their goggles or computer and teach their students with the material we provide or custom make for their class.
